Introduction

The modern mental health crisis in the United States has taken a toll on all individuals across the country. With more people suffering from psychological disorders than ever before in the United States, Mental health care is at a premium. While inpatient and outpatient mental health facilities have undoubtedly achieved successful diagnosis and treatment of a range of disorders, clinical resources including psychiatrists, psychologists, and social workers are spread thin and overworked (Modi et al., 2022). With expanding networks and growing needs in United States mental health care, effective and precise triage of individuals with mental illness is of paramount importance in order to deliver care to those who need it most. The present analysis seeks to understand if machine learning may be applied to better triage those with severe mental health conditions in need of service. The following questions are assessed herein: Can a machine learning approach effectively triage individuals suffering from Serious Mental Illnesses? Moreover, What variables are most useful in predicting deleterious mental health outcomes? To investigate these questions, I began my investigation at the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Service Administration (SAMHSA) website https://www.samhsa.gov/. SAMHSA performs nationally representative surveys of mental health services, symptoms, and substance abuse in the United States on an annual basis. The administration publishes five nationwide surveys per year, three of which focus on mental health and substance abuse services in the United States to measure the effectiveness of clinics and public/private funding, while the other twothe Mental Health Client-Level Data (MH-CLD) and The National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH) focus on individual health indicators in such facilities. In an effort to effectively work with particular demographic and general diagnostic information with a manageable range, I chose to use the MH-CLD, containing 68 survey questions, rather than the NSDUH, which contains over 200 items many of which concern the specifics of drug usage, which may be out of the scope of the current research question. The MH-CLD releases yearly public-use files (PUFs), treated with confidentiality protections. These PUFs contain information about clients who have received mental health treatment services, including demographics, national outcome measures, and substance use characteristics. The codebook for the data can be obtained here: https://www.samhsa.gov/data/data-we-collect/mh-cld/datafiles. In this way, the MH-CLD represents the most user-friendly sample of nationally collected data publicly available. For the present analysis, my chosen target variable for prediction will be SMI/SED status, which is coded in the data as SMISED. This variable indicates whether an individual client has had a serious mental illness (SMI) or serious emotional disturbance (SED) using the NIMH definition. According to the NIMH, an SMI represents a subset of Any Mental Illness (AMI) where a mental, behavioral, or emotional disorder results in serious functional impairment, which substantially interferes with or limits one or more major life activities. In particular, the burden of mental illnesses is especially concentrated among those who experience disability due to SMI (NIMH). Childhood SED is defined as the presence of a diagnosable mental, behavioral, or emotional disorder that results in functional impairment that substantially interferes with or limits the childs role or functioning in family, school, or community activities (SAMHSA, 1993). According to these definitions, SED may be thought of as the childhood equivalent of SMI, due to the limitations of many diagnoses for minors in the United States. Those with SMI/SED in the present data set represent a group that ought to receive more individualized and effective care. An effective machine learning algorithm would fit into a stepped care model which seeks to prioritize a communitys finite mental health resources to those who may be struggling the most (Ho et al., 2016). Moreover, if we can understand what demographic and diagnostic factors contribute to particularly debilitating mental health outcomes, we may better implement early childhood interventions for those in at-risk communities. While this sample represents those who have received either inpatient or outpatient care from a mental health provider, these individuals can provide the grounds for those in the real world who have yet to receive or seek help.

Support Vector Machine

To predict severe mental health outcomes, I opted to first try a Support Vector Machine (SVM). A SVM is a supervised learning approach that is well suited for classification problems with high dimensionality such as the one at hand. In a SVM, all data points coordinates are refereed to as features in the n-dimensional space. In essence a SVM takes the data set and attempts to draw a line that subsets datapoints into two classes. This line, known as a hyperplane has the goal of placing all points of one class on one side (e.g.all SMI/SED individuals) and others (no SMI/SED) on the other side. While there could be a number of appropriate hyperplanes, SVM tries to find the one that best differentiates the two categories. This is done by calculating the line at which the distance between points in both classes is at its maximum. Below is the best fit SVM application for this data set.

set.seed(1)
training_rows <- sample(1:nrow(completed_CLD_sample),
                        size = nrow(completed_CLD_sample)/2)

Random Forest

Next, I compared the results of this SVM with those of a random forest. As opposed to the SVM, the random forest seeks to make prediction off of a collection of decision trees to make predictions

set.seed(77)
random_f <- randomForest(as.factor(SMISED) ~ . -YEAR -CASEID, data = train_data)
rf_predictions <- predict(random_f, test_data)

# confusion matrix
table(rf_predictions, train_data$SMISED)
##                
## rf_predictions  No SMI or SED SMI/SED
##   No SMI or SED           245     708
##   SMI/SED                1044    3003

The above confusion matrix represents my chosen random forest model. Similar to the SVM above, false positive rate was high, however, only 20 % compared to 24% for the SVM. Overall accuracy was down to 60% in the random forest model as compared to 70% in th SVM.Most importantly, however, false negatives were up relative to the SVM at a mark of 14.16 % as compared to 6.86%. This difference can not be understated in a medical context. The price of a false positive in this context would involve an individual who is not at risk for SMI/SED being given mental health resources. While the purpose of the present analysis is to ease the burden of mental health professionals, the opposite scenario is much worse. A false negative implies that an individual who is likely to have SMI/SED is not properly triaged and therefore not given adequate mental health care. This effect would lead to complications at a greater scale if the random forest model was used over the SVM. Since false negatives are more costly in a medical setting, and accuracy is additionally higher in the SVM, I would chose the SVM to triage clients.

Conclusions

In all, the CLD-MH provided some key insights into factors contributing to severe mental illness, however, this survey understandably represents a limited window into the multi-dimensional issue of human behavioral health. In response to the first question proposed, machine learning may be able to effectively triage those at risk, however, the present analysis demonstrates the difficulty of forecasting mental illness severity. The accuracy of both attempted models was less than 70%, which each holding a large burden of false negative results. These results are likely due to the multiple interactions between variables in the dataset, and the invsible data points such as genetic predispition and SES that are not accouted for in the MH-CLD survey. It would be convenient to conclude that the data points with the highest importance are those that should be of additionally emphasis for collecting. However, geographic location and age do not reveal any additional layers of an individuals predisposition for severe mental illness. The most important conclusion that may be drawn is the importance of primary diagnoses. As mentioned previously, those acute diagnoses carry additional weight for functional disruption down the line. This analysis emphasis the need for effective diagnoses and early identification and intervention for acute mental illness. However, it seems as though the present analysis has pointed more toward the effect of geography than the present demographic information. According to our random forest model, the state that a sampled client lived in was the single best predictor of SMI/SED. Moreover, state, geographic division, and geographic region were all better predictors than variables such as educational attainment, race, ethnicity, gender. These findings are contrary to expectation. Future research would profit from investigating the geographical disparities that were indicated in this report. Additionally, an important class of variables to add to similar analyses to potentially control for the outsized effect of geography would be SES indicators. In addition to a limited range of questions in the MH-CLD, the present analysis is limited as it only considers collected data for those in facilities. there is a definite bias toward those with both more severe conditions and those who are generally help seeking. In this way, generalizations to those undiagnosed in community settings is limited.
